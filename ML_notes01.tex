	\section{Vectors, Vector Spaces and Vector Subspaces}
	\subsection{Vectors}
	The primitive idea of a vector is that, it is a quantity with magnitude and direction. But the abstract idea of a vector is that, it can be thought of as an ordered n-tuple. The way we interpret that, can be based on different applications of that vector. Suppose, we have a 5-component vector like, \[
	u = \begin{pmatrix}
		u_1 \\
		u_2 \\
		u_3 \\
		u_4 \\
		u_5
	\end{pmatrix}
	\]
	where each of the five components represent a health parameter of a patient. E.g. blood pressure, pulse rate etc. This idea is strictly tied to the application of the vector. In order to define the vector mathematically, we need an abstract concept of \textbf{`Field'}, that will lead us to the \textbf{`Vectors'}.
	\subsection{Field $\mathbb{F}$}
	The Field is a non-empty collection of elements with operations ($+$, $\cdot$) where $+$ is the Field Addition and $\cdot$ is the Field Multiplication, such that the following properties hold: 
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item There exists $0 \in \mathbb{F}$ such that for any $a \in \mathbb{F}$, $a + 0 = 0 + a = a$. $0$ is the Additive Identity element here.
		\item For any two elements $a,b \in \mathbb{F}$, $a + b \in \mathbb{F}$.
		\item There exists for every element $a \in \mathbb{F}$, the Additive Inverse $(-a) \in \mathbb{F}$, such that $a + (-a) = 0$.
		\item There exists $1 \in \mathbb{F}$ such that for any $a \in \mathbb{F}$, $a \cdot 1 = 1 \cdot a = a$. $1$ is the Multiplicative Identity element here.
		\item For any two elements $a,b \in \mathbb{F}$, $a \cdot b \in \mathbb{F}$.
		\item There exists for every non-zero element $a \in \mathbb{F}$, the Multiplicative Inverse $a^{-1} \in \mathbb{F}$, such that $a \cdot a^{-1} = a^{-1} \cdot a = 1$.
	\end{enumerate}
	If these six properties are obeyed by a set of elements, then that set is called a Field, especially defined by the two operations: Field Addition and Field Multiplication.\newline
	
	\noindent \textbf{Example 1:} Let us consider the set of Real Numbers $\mathbb{R}$.
	
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item $0 \in \mathbb{R}$ such that $1 \in \mathbb{R}$, $0 + 1 = 1 + 0 = 1$. 
		\item $(2,3) \in \mathbb{R}$ such that $2 + 3 = 5 \in \mathbb{R}$.
		\item $(2, -2) \in \mathbb{R}$ such that $ 2 + (-2) = 0$
		\item $1 \in \mathbb{R}$ such that $2 \in \mathbb{R}, 2 \cdot 1 = 1 \cdot 2 = 2$
		\item $(2,3) \in \mathbb{R}$ such that $2 \cdot 3 = 6 \in \mathbb{R}$.
		\item $(2, \frac{1}{2}) \in \mathbb{R}$ such that $ 2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 1$
	\end{enumerate}
	Since, six of the properties are being satisfied by the set of Real Numbers $\mathbb{R}$, hence $\mathbb{R}$ is a Field.
	
	\newpage
	\noindent \textbf{Example 2:} Let us consider the set of Integers $\mathbb{Z}$.
	
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item $0 \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $1 \in \mathbb{Z}$, $0 + 1 = 1 + 0 = 1$. 
		\item $(2,3) \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $2 + 3 = 5 \in \mathbb{Z}$.
		\item $(2, -2) \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $ 2 + (-2) = 0$
		\item $1 \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $2 \in \mathbb{Z}, 2 \cdot 1 = 1 \cdot 2 = 2$
		\item $(2,3) \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $2 \cdot 3 = 6 \in \mathbb{Z}$.
		\item For $2 \in \mathbb{Z}$, the multiplicative inverse is $\frac{1}{2}$, but $\frac{1}{2} \notin \mathbb{Z}$.
	\end{enumerate}
	Since the sixth property (the existence of a multiplicative inverse) is violated by the set of Integers $\mathbb{Z}$, hence $\mathbb{Z}$ is \textbf{not} a Field.
	
	\vspace{0.5em}
	
	\noindent \textbf{Example 3:} Let us consider the set of Integers modulo 5, denoted as $\mathbb{Z}_5 = \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4\}$, where all operations are performed modulo 5.
	
	\vspace{1em}
	\noindent To easily visualize all possible combinations for these operations in $\mathbb{Z}_5$, we can construct addition and multiplication tables:
	
	\begin{center}
		% --- Left Table (Addition) ---
		\begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\textwidth}
			\centering
			\textbf{Addition Modulo 5 ($+$)} \\[0.5em]
			\begin{tabular}{c|ccccc}
				$+$ & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\ \hline
				0   & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\
				1   & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 0 \\
				2   & 2 & 3 & 4 & 0 & 1 \\
				3   & 3 & 4 & 0 & 1 & 2 \\
				4   & 4 & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 \\
			\end{tabular}
		\end{minipage}
		\hfill % This pushes the second table smoothly to the right side
		% --- Right Table (Multiplication) ---
		\begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\textwidth}
			\centering
			\textbf{Multiplication Modulo 5 ($\cdot$)} \\[0.5em]
			\begin{tabular}{c|ccccc}
				$\cdot$ & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\ \hline
				0       & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
				1       & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\
				2       & 0 & 2 & 4 & 1 & 3 \\
				3       & 0 & 3 & 1 & 4 & 2 \\
				4       & 0 & 4 & 3 & 2 & 1 \\
			\end{tabular}
		\end{minipage}
	\end{center}
	
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item $0 \in \mathbb{Z}_5$ such that $1 \in \mathbb{Z}_5$, $0 + 1 = 1 + 0 = 1 \pmod 5$. 
		\item $(2,3) \in \mathbb{Z}_5$ such that $2 + 3 = 5 \equiv 0 \in \mathbb{Z}_5$.
		\item $(2, 3) \in \mathbb{Z}_5$ such that $2 + 3 = 5 \equiv 0$, meaning $3$ is the additive inverse of $2$ (since $-2 \equiv 3 \pmod 5$).
		\item $1 \in \mathbb{Z}_5$ such that $2 \in \mathbb{Z}_5, 2 \cdot 1 = 1 \cdot 2 = 2 \pmod 5$.
		\item $(2,3) \in \mathbb{Z}_5$ such that $2 \cdot 3 = 6 \equiv 1 \in \mathbb{Z}_5$.
		\item $(2, 3) \in \mathbb{Z}_5$ such that $2 \cdot 3 = 6 \equiv 1$, meaning $3$ is the multiplicative inverse of $2$ (since $2^{-1} \equiv 3 \pmod 5$).
	\end{enumerate}
	Since all six properties are perfectly satisfied under modular arithmetic, the finite set $\mathbb{Z}_5$ is a Field (specifically, a finite field).
	
	\vspace{0.5em}
	
	\noindent \textbf{Example 4:} Let us consider the set of Integers modulo 6, denoted as $\mathbb{Z}_6 = \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$, under modular addition and multiplication.
	
	\begin{center}
		% --- Left Table (Addition Modulo 6) ---
		\begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\textwidth}
			\centering
			\textbf{Addition Modulo 6 ($+$)} \\[0.5em]
			\begin{tabular}{c|cccccc}
				$+$ & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 \\ \hline
				0   & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 \\
				1   & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 & 0 \\
				2   & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 & 0 & 1 \\
				3   & 3 & 4 & 5 & 0 & 1 & 2 \\
				4   & 4 & 5 & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 \\
				5   & 5 & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\
			\end{tabular}
		\end{minipage}
		\hfill % Pushes the second table smoothly to the right side
		% --- Right Table (Multiplication Modulo 6) ---
		\begin{minipage}[t]{0.45\textwidth}
			\centering
			\textbf{Multiplication Modulo 6 ($\cdot$)} \\[0.5em]
			\begin{tabular}{c|cccccc}
				$\cdot$ & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 \\ \hline
				0       & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
				1       & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 \\
				2       & 0 & 2 & 4 & 0 & 2 & 4 \\
				3       & 0 & 3 & 0 & 3 & 0 & 3 \\
				4       & 0 & 4 & 2 & 0 & 4 & 2 \\
				5       & 0 & 5 & 4 & 3 & 2 & 1 \\
			\end{tabular}
		\end{minipage}
	\end{center}
	
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item $0 \in \mathbb{Z}_6$ such that $1 \in \mathbb{Z}_6$, $0 + 1 = 1 + 0 = 1 \pmod 6$. 
		\item $(2,5) \in \mathbb{Z}_6$ such that $2 + 5 = 7 \equiv 1 \in \mathbb{Z}_6$.
		\item $(2, 4) \in \mathbb{Z}_6$ such that $2 + 4 = 6 \equiv 0$, meaning $4$ is the additive inverse of $2$.
		\item $1 \in \mathbb{Z}_6$ such that $2 \in \mathbb{Z}_6, 2 \cdot 1 = 1 \cdot 2 = 2 \pmod 6$.
		\item $(2,4) \in \mathbb{Z}_6$ such that $2 \cdot 4 = 8 \equiv 2 \in \mathbb{Z}_6$.
		\item \textbf{Failure Condition:} Consider the non-zero element $2 \in \mathbb{Z}_6$. If we look at its multiples:
		\begin{align*}
			2 \cdot 0 &\equiv 0, \quad 2 \cdot 1 \equiv 2, \quad 2 \cdot 2 \equiv 4 \\
			2 \cdot 3 &\equiv 0, \quad 2 \cdot 4 \equiv 2, \quad 2 \cdot 5 \equiv 4
		\end{align*}
		There is no element $x \in \mathbb{Z}_6$ such that $2 \cdot x \equiv 1$. Therefore, $2$ has no multiplicative inverse ($2^{-1}$ does not exist).
	\end{enumerate}
	Since the sixth property (existence of a multiplicative inverse for all non-zero elements) is violated, $\mathbb{Z}_6$ is \textbf{not} a Field.\newline
	
	\noindent As we can see, even though $\mathbb{Z}_5$ is a field, but $\mathbb{Z}_6$ is not. In order to generalize this for $\mathbb{Z}_n$, it is only a field, when $n$ is a prime number.
